Where's Paul

This work is the result of 2 assignments given by John Wood as part of the UI Design module (which was part of my Msc in Creative Digital Media). Initial conception and sketching were done in teams. My team members were David Knox, Sanette Tanaka and Zoe Stringer.

Read all the way down to interact with a live prototype!

Brief

The Support Team for Widget Consulting needs to be notified each morning how each employee is about to spend the working day: off sick, on leave, working from home, on site or on a business trip abroad. Since many employees don’t notify the support team on their whereabouts and neglect to update their calendar, the support team has to spend time each morning trying to track down all employees who are not at the office. This creates an additional problem where employees don’t know where their co workers are and whether they can be reached. The brief asks to create a mobile app for Widget Consulting that would assist primarily the Support Team (but also all other staff members) in knowing what is the daily status for each employee.

Insight

By interrogating the brief, and based on staff interviews, we detected 4 major challenges which the UI has to address. The following is a table detailing the challenges and the solutions we found for them.

Employees view the task of informing about their whereabouts as tedious and not worthwhile. Hence, updating one’s status has to be as quick and pain-free as possible.
  • Daily push notification that the user can handle straight from the notifications panel, with just one click.
  • Since the need was to give visibility to employee’s availability, and not location, we reduced the number of options from 5 to 3
  • Whenever possible, the app will spare the need to tag oneself (i.e will not send push notifications) and tag the employee automatically as "in the office” in the following cases:
    • the app detected the office’s wifi.
    • the app detected the employee had logged into their office computer.
  • The app should allow the user to set long, future, abstinences. This will prevent the user from receiving a daily notification while on their vacation (for example).
  • Signing up and setting a user profile should be as pain-free as possible. Since all employees use company gmail accounts, the app would use Google authentication and would use the user’s picture and name from the Google account.
Employees are interested in knowing about their co-workers’ availability
  • The app should allow you to go inside and get a (filtered) list of people’s statuses.
  • Use employees’ interest in knowing about their co-workers’ availability by implementing a reciprocity mechanism: You need to enter yours to see others’.
Employees’ location might change throughout the day (ex. an employee starts the day at the office but goes to meetings outside at lunchtime).
  • Allow employees to ping each other. Conforming to the principle of simplicity, there will be no option for text messaging. Just polite pinging.
  • Allow the user to write a custom status (i.e. “meeting with clients during the morning. will be at the office by 13:00.”).
There is a need for an administrator for the support team.
  • The Support Team would receive administrator permissions and would be able to: remove an employee (in case they quit working), remove an employee’s picture (in case it is inappropriate), export data into excel (for legal use).

Design Process

My group's initial concept used a "river of cards" layout for displaying coworkers’ availability. Each employee card, when tapped, would display additional details about that person’s availability and would allow the user to ping them. However, as I progressed through the process of wireframing and prototyping it became evident that a traditional list layout would make for a preferable layout: with a list, the user would be able to perform all major tasks straight from the list-view, without having to tap on a colleague’s card to reveal those options.

Prototype

Following sketching, I built this high-fidelity prototype, to be used in future usability testings. It was designed for Android 5 and adheres to Material Design guidelines. Graphics were created using Adobe Illustrator, and the prototyping tool I used is InVision. Why not have a try!

Swoosh!

Swoosh! is a casual mobile game I created together with my teammate David Harrington, as part of my masters. We worked on the game for an entire semester, presenting our progress and receiving feedback each week. I was in charge of the technical development of the game, whereas David was in charge of the visual assets, art style and audio.

TL;DR - We created a mobile game in 12 weeks. Our game was based on the mechanics of an existing game called "Dragon, Fly!". We were successful in overcoming a major technical issue (lack of pixel-perfect collision in GameSalad) and completed the project on time.

The Brief

Our task was to pick an existing mobile game and create an imitation of it.
Since the focus of that module was to practice agile development and teamwork (through the creation of a game), we were requested to build the game using the GameSalad authoring tool, as to remove the obstacle of native coding.

We had 12 weeks to develop, design and publish the game on the play store.

Decisions, decisions...

We chose to imitate a lovely game called "Dragon, Fly!", in which the player plays a baby dragon who escapes from its mother. Being too small to fly, the baby dragon "swooshes" through valleys, gaining momentum with every successful swoosh. Once the mother catches up with it, the game is over. The aim of the game is to progress as much as one can before getting caught.

The game essentially consists of a curvy terrain of hills and valleys, and a hero swooshing between them. Sounds simple enough, right? We thought so too.

Watch the baby dragon in action.

Our interpretation

We decided to recreate "Dragon, Fly!" as a Halloween themed game. Instead of a baby dragon escaping its mother, we had a witch escaping an angry mob.

Instructions for our version of the game.

Who knew that pixel-collision would become the object of my desires?...

It soon became evident that we had chosen the wrong game-concept for the GameSalad platform. You see, GameSalad does not support pixel collision, meaning we had no way of making our witch collide perfectly with the curvy terrain of the mountains. By the time we realized the severity of this problem it was too late to select a different game. We searched online forums far and wide for a solution to our issue, only to read again and again that it simply can't be done in GameSalad.

Fun, right? I could watch that all day.

Who cares about pixel-perfect collision anyway?

GameSalad treats all objects as either rectangle or circle shaped. This often makes for awkward situations when those objects touch (collide). We would have much prefered to have pixel-perfect collision, which would mean for the game engine to treat the object as the irregular shapes that they are and allow for a more natural and precise collision between them, based on their real boundaries.

A member of the GameSalad community named Manto found a workaround for the pixel collision issue and had posted their solution on the GameSalad forum. Essentially, his idea was to create lots of tiny transparent rectangles, and place them side by side in slightly different angles along the conture of the terrain image, in a way that would create the illusion of one consecutive curvy line. Since these were all individual (albeit tiny) linear surfaces, an object (say, a swooshing witch) could collide with each of them.

This solution still was far from sufficiant for us (read on to find out why), but it gave us hope and inspiration.

The smooth-looking terrain is actually made out of many tiny rectangles

The battle is far from over

Since our game is a never-ending horizontal scroll game, having a curvy line all the way through would necessitate creating of lots and lots of those rectangular-shaped objects ("actors" in GameSalad speak), which proved to be too costly in terms of memory and resulted in very poor performance. For example, in order to create just one (36 mountains long) level, we needed to have about 1,080 rectangle actors! In addition, since positioning these rectangles manually was not feasible, I had to come up with a way of shaping them into mountains automatically.

Maths to the rescue!

Firstly, in order to avoid the need to manually place all of the tiny lines as to create the mountainy terrain, I created a tool in Excel that would allow me to define formulas for particular "mountain" shapes and then to generate the coordinates for those. I used a sequence of sine waves with different amplitude and wavelength to create different mountains. Then I imported the coordinates into GameSalad, and created the complementary logic to randomly compose a terrain out of those "mountains", with an increasing level of difficulty for each level.

I used Excel to create coordinates for the terrain.

It's not a bug, it's a feature

Since creating the entire mountainy terrain in advances (when the game loads) necessitated having thousands and thousands of tiny rectangles which were weighing down performance, my second challenge was to reduce the number of rectangles used at any given moment to the bare minimum. This technical necessity made me rethink the very design of the game, and I eventually decided to make the terrain gradually appear as the gameplay progressed. This feature was well received in testing, as players commented that the constantly advancing terrain adds a sense of magic, as well as making for a slightly more challenging experience (since the player cannot plan far ahead for where they would land the witch).

Develop, test, make changes, repeat...

Throughout development and graphic design, we have tested our game rigorously, and made changes accordingly. Most notable changes we made had to do with the witch's movement (speed and height of leaping), choice of music, and color and contrast.

Result

We have completed our game and published it on the play store.
